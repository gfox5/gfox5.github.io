Snowboarding

All information and images on this page were sourced from the snowboarding Wikipedia page.

Snowboarding is both a recreational and competitive winter sport that was first introduced to the Olympics in 1998. It involves descending a snowy surface with the rider's feet attached to a snowboard. It's popularity in the United States peaked in 2007. While it's popularity has declined slightly, it is still enjoyed by many, and will be featured at the 2026 Winter Olympics.

History

Snowboards were first developed in 1965 when an engineer in Muskegon, Michigan named Sherm Poppen created a toy for his kids. This device was two skis fastened together with a rope attached to one end. Him and his wife, Nancy, called the invention the "snurfer" and sold the licensing to a manufacturer. In 1966 alone, over half a million snurfers were sold.

image source

Modern snowboards were invented by Tom Sims and Jake Burton Carpenter. Sims created his first snowboard by gluing carpet to the top of a piece of wood, and aluminum to the bottom. He started producing snowboards commercially in the 1970s. Burton was an avid snurfer. At the age of 14, he competed at the Michigan snurfing competition and impressed the crowd with his own self-designed bindings.

As the sport gained traction in the 1970s and 1980s, pioneers came up with new designs for boards and bindings. As the technology developed, snowboarders could achieve greater feats and do more advanced tricks. The first USA National Snowboard race was held near Woodstock, Vermont at Suicide Six.

Safety

Similar to other extreme sports, there are risks associated with snowboarding.

Snowboarders face a greature risk of fracture than skiiers, especially in the upper extremeties. However, the risk of injury to the lower extremities is much lower. In comparison to skiiers, the risk of injury to snowboarders is around five per thousand people per day, which is double the injury rate for skiiers. Beginners are more likely to experience injury, especially if they do not take lessons with an experienced instructor. In fact, one quarter of all injuries are accquired by first-timers, and one half of all injuries happen to those with less than one year's experience. While beginners are more prone to injury, experienced riders tend to get more severe injuries.

Common Injuries

Head Injury
Two to six times greater risk for snowboarders than skiiers. Most likely to occur when carrying out a heelside turn. Failure in doing so results in landing on the backside and slamming the head into the ground.
Wrist Injury
Forty percent of all snowboarding related injuries are to the wrists, with 100,000 wrist fractures each year worldwide.
Knee Injury
Fifteen percent of snowboarding injuries are knee related, specifically to the knee ligaments.
Ankle Injury
While lower leg and ankle injuries are rare, injury to the talus bone accounts for 2% of all snowboarding injuries. It is commonly misdiagnosed as a sprain, but some medical professionals refer to this injury as "snowboarder's ankle".
Injuries in the Lift Line
Many injuries take place while waiting for or getting off of the chairlift. This is due to the fact that snowboarders will push themselves through the line with one foot strapped in, while the other is free. This is most likely to result in knee injuries to the leading foot.

Precautions to Take

Helmet
It is strongly recommended to wear a helmet while partaking in winter sports. Even if you are a cautious rider, it is important to realize that other people on the mountain may not be. Helments significantly reduce the risk of head injury.
Protective Eyewear
Eye Injury can occur from impact and exposure to bright light in snow covered areas. Wearing eye protection can protect from snow blindness and injury to the eyes.
Lessons
Taking a lesson with an experienced rider can greatly decrease risk of injury on your first ride. An instructure will provide expertise and technical knowledge, such as the correct way to fall, and how to use your edges.
Avalanche Training
Avalanches are very common in alpine environments. Avalanche teams at ski resorts take precautions so that they are less likely to occur within bounds. It is still important to have knowledge on what to do in such event and how to prevent one's causation, especially if riding in the backcountry.
Equipment
It is important to fit yourself with the correct equipment for riding. Snowboard boots should fit snugly with the toes touching the front of the boot when standing up straight. Padding is strongly recommended on parts of the body such as hips, knees, shoulders, teeth, and butt.
Riding Alone
Many experts advise against riding alone especially around dense trees. When there is lots of snow on the ground, there is a high risk of encountering tree wells.

Culture

Early on in snowboarding history, there was a rebellion against skiing culture. Skiiers were hesitant to accept snowboarders on the slopes. Snowboarding culture became very similar to skateboarding and surfing culture. Early snowboarders incorporated urban and punk styles into their look and attitude. Words such as "dude", "gnarly", and "Shred the Gnar" emerged from snowboarding culture.

Terminology

  • Stances

    The way that a snowboarder positions their feet on the board.

    • Goofy: riding with right foot forward
    • Regular: riding with left foot forward
    • Switch: riding in one's opposite stance

  • Edges

    • Heel Edge: The edge of the board that the heels rest on
    • Toe Edge: The edge of the board that the toes rest on
